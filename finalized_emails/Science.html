China is building a space telescope to rival the JWST — and it could survive in orbit decades longer - Livescience.com

China's space agency is developing the China Space Station Telescope (CSST), a powerful new observatory designed to rival the James Webb Space Telescope. CSST, also known as Xuntian, will boast advanced optics granting it a field of view 300 times greater than Hubble's, and will operate across ultraviolet to near-infrared wavelengths.

Scheduled for launch no earlier than 2026, CSST will join a group of cutting-edge telescopes, including the Euclid, Roman, and Vera C. Rubin observatories. These telescopes share a common goal: to unveil cosmic mysteries through deep-space surveys.

CSST's capabilities will enable measurements of weak gravitational lensing, providing insights into dark matter distribution. It will also study cosmic voids and galaxy clusters to shed light on dark energy. Furthermore, CSST will hunt for supernovas and examine baryon acoustic oscillations, both crucial for understanding the universe's evolution.

With its orbit shared with the Tiangong space station, CSST will have the unique ability to be serviced, upgraded, and potentially operate for decades, marking a significant advancement in space-based astronomy.

Black Hole at The Center of Our Galaxy Seen 'Bubbling' With Activity - ScienceAlert

NASA's James Webb Space Telescope has revealed a surprisingly active central region in our Milky Way galaxy.

New observations show that Sagittarius A*, the supermassive black hole at the galaxy's core, is constantly experiencing bursts and flares of light. These bursts vary in size and duration, happening every few hours as hot gas in the accretion disk surrounding the black hole is disrupted.

Scientists believe the flares are triggered by turbulence in the accretion disk, similar to solar flares, and by powerful magnetic reconnection events. These findings provide unique insights into the dynamic processes happening near a black hole and could help researchers better understand these enigmatic objects.

This New Drug Mimics The Health Effects of Living at High Altitude - ScienceAlert

Scientists have developed a new drug called HypoxyStat that mimics the benefits of breathing in thin, high-altitude air. This drug could be life-saving for people with metabolic diseases like Leigh syndrome, which is characterized by the body's inability to use oxygen efficiently.

HypoxyStat works by mimicking the body's natural adaptation to high altitudes, where oxygen levels are lower. Studies in mice with Leigh syndrome showed that HypoxyStat significantly reduced brain damage, extended lifespan, and even reversed existing damage.

This innovative drug offers a potential solution for patients who cannot move to high-altitude environments, providing a controlled and safe way to access the therapeutic benefits of low oxygen. Researchers are now working on a next-generation version of the drug for further testing in primates and potential human trials.

NASA has selected SpaceX to launch the NEO Surveyor space telescope, a mission focused on detecting dangerous asteroids near Earth.

The telescope, scheduled to launch in September 2027 aboard a Falcon 9 rocket, will be stationed at the Earth-sun L-1 Lagrange point. Equipped with a powerful infrared telescope, NEO Surveyor aims to identify and track near-Earth objects (NEOs) larger than 140 meters in diameter.

This critical mission highlights the importance of constantly monitoring NEOs, as recently demonstrated by asteroid 2024 YR4, which initially posed a threat before further observations significantly reduced the risk of an impact.

James Webb Telescope Unveils a Mysterious Dwarf Galaxy That Shouldn’t Exist - Indian Defence Review

The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) has captured an image of Leo P, a dwarf galaxy 5.3 million light-years away, revealing a surprising resurgence of star formation. This diminutive galaxy, largely composed of hydrogen and helium, is believed to be a pristine example of how galaxies formed in the early universe. Its isolation from other galaxies has allowed it to retain its original characteristics, making it a valuable subject for studying galaxy evolution.

What makes Leo P particularly remarkable is its unexpected revival of star birth after a long period of dormancy. This is unusual for dwarf galaxies, which often cease forming stars due to a lack of necessary materials. JWST's image shows clusters of bright blue stars, indicating recent star formation, as well as a bubble-like structure of ionized hydrogen, further confirming this phenomenon.

Scientists believe Leo P went through phases of initial star formation, followed by a lengthy dormancy, and now a third phase of renewed star formation. This unique history offers insights into the early universe and the role of dwarf galaxies. Its ancient composition and isolated location make it a time capsule, providing clues about the conditions present during the Epoch of Reionization, a crucial period in cosmic history when the first stars and galaxies began shaping the universe.
